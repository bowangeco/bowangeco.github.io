This research examines how long-distance biotic interactions reshape coastal wetland structure and resilience. We focused on the Yancheng coastal wetlands along the Yellow Sea, where the native red marsh formed by <em>Suaeda salsa</em> has high ecological, cultural, and conservation value. These red marshes support diverse coastal communities and provide important habitats for migratory waterbirds, but they have declined rapidly in recent decades.

<figure style="margin-top: 24px; margin-bottom: 28px;">
  <img src="/images/research/1_Coastal_Fig1.PNG" alt="Landscape transition in Yancheng coastal wetlands from 1982 to 2020" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    The Yancheng coastal wetland originally showed a clear intertidal vegetation zonation: unvegetated mudflats at low elevations, native <em>Suaeda salsa</em> red marshes in the middle marsh, and native <em>Phragmites australis</em> reed marshes at higher elevations. After the invasion of <em>Spartina alterniflora</em>, the landscape underwent a major transition over nearly four decades. <em>Spartina</em> expanded in the low marsh and then became relatively spatially stabilized, whereas <em>Phragmites</em> expanded landward and the native <em>Suaeda</em> red marsh declined sharply. This pattern suggests that the loss of <em>Suaeda</em> was not simply caused by direct replacement by <em>Spartina</em>, but by a broader landscape-scale reorganization.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

<figure style="margin-top: 24px; margin-bottom: 28px;">
  <img src="/images/research/1_Coastal_Fig2.PNG" alt="Conceptual mechanism of long-distance interaction triggered by Spartina invasion" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    We proposed that <em>Spartina</em> invasion triggered a long-distance interaction by changing the physical environment of the coastal landscape. Establishment of <em>Spartina</em> in the low marsh enhanced sediment accumulation and raised local elevation. This elevated low-marsh zone reduced tidal inundation and salt input to landward habitats, leading to lower soil salinity in the former <em>Suaeda</em> zone. Because <em>Suaeda</em> depends on hypersaline middle-marsh conditions as a competitive refuge, this desalination weakened its advantage and facilitated the expansion of <em>Phragmites</em>. In this way, an invasive species located in the low marsh indirectly reshaped vegetation dynamics several kilometers away.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

<figure style="margin-top: 24px; margin-bottom: 28px;">
  <img src="/images/research/1_Coastal_Fig3.PNG" alt="Modeling long-distance interactions and coastal vegetation resilience" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    To test this mechanism, we combined remote sensing, field observations, and a spatial vegetation model. The model reproduced the observed changes in soil salinity, vegetation cover, and spatial niches across the decadal study period. It further showed that long-distance facilitation between <em>Spartina</em> and <em>Phragmites</em> can determine the outcome of vegetation succession: without this interaction, the three vegetation types could coexist across the landscape; with this interaction, the system shifts toward a two-species state dominated by <em>Spartina</em> and <em>Phragmites</em>, with strong loss of <em>Suaeda</em>. The model also predicted that future sea-level rise may alter this trajectory by increasing salinity and partially improving the persistence of native <em>Suaeda</em> marshes, although their long-term fate remains uncertain.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

Overall, this work reveals that plant species can interact across spatial scales of up to approximately 10 km and temporal scales of several decades. Such long-distance interactions can amplify the ecological consequences of biological invasions, reorganize coastal habitat zonation, and reshape ecosystem resilience under future sea-level rise. More broadly, the study shows how local biotic processes can scale up to drive landscape-level ecosystem structure, offering a clearer view of how coastal ecosystems respond to invasion, environmental change, and spatially extended feedbacks.
