title: "Dryland multistability under human water-resource regulation"

This research examines how human water-resource regulation reshapes multistability and resilience in dryland ecosystems. Using the Hexi Corridor in northwestern China as a model system, we investigated how human activities alter ecosystem dynamics beyond the constraints imposed by natural water availability.

  <img src="/images/research/2_Hexi_Figure8.png" alt="Hexi Corridor as a typical dryland social-ecological system" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    The Hexi Corridor is a typical dryland social-ecological system, where ecosystems are strongly constrained by water limitation and increasingly shaped by human water use. Although precipitation did not show a clear increasing trend and potential evapotranspiration tended to rise, vegetation greenness increased markedly over the past two decades. The consistency between increasing NDVI and rising water use suggests that human water-resource regulation may have become a dominant driver of recent ecosystem change.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

<figure style="margin-top: 24px; margin-bottom: 28px;">
  <img src="/images/research/2_Hexi_Figure7.png" alt="Conceptual framework of human water regulation and dryland multistability" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    We proposed that, beyond the original dynamics of natural dryland ecosystems, human activities can create a new feedback mechanism by regulating water-resource redistribution. Water subsidy through irrigation, ecological water allocation, and socio-economic development may improve ecosystem quality and promote the formation and maintenance of a high-quality ecosystem state that would be difficult to sustain under natural water limitation alone.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

<figure style="margin-top: 24px; margin-bottom: 28px;">
  <img src="/images/research/2_Hexi_Figure3.png" alt="Potential landscape analysis of dryland multistability with and without human activity" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    Based on potential landscape analysis, we found a new ecosystem state that is largely independent of the original bistability of the natural dryland system. Without human activity, ecosystem quality remains mainly constrained by natural water availability and is concentrated in a low-quality state. When human activity is incorporated, a distinct high-quality ecosystem state emerges, indicating that human water-resource regulation can drive the formation of a substantially improved ecosystem state.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

<figure style="margin-top: 24px; margin-bottom: 28px;">
  <img src="/images/research/2_Hexi_Figure5.png" alt="Water-use sectors driving the high-quality ecosystem state" style="width:100%; border-radius:8px; display:block;">
  <figcaption style="font-size:0.9em; line-height:1.5; color:#555; margin-top:8px;">
    By linking ecosystem quality with sectoral water use, we found that forestry irrigation and environmental water supply are the two most important positive contributors to the formation of the high-quality ecosystem state. These results suggest that targeted water redistribution, rather than total water use alone, plays a critical role in shaping dryland ecosystem stability and resilience.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

Beyond these findings, this research further shows that the high-quality ecosystem state maintained by human water-resource regulation may become increasingly vulnerable under future climate warming. As evaporative demand rises, sustaining this state may require greater water allocation to ecological restoration and vegetation maintenance. Without sufficient water support, the system may face increasing risk of degradation or transition back toward a low-quality dryland state.
