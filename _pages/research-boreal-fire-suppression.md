---
title: "Fire suppression effects on bistability and resilience in boreal forest–grassland systems"
permalink: /research/boreal-fire-suppression/
author_profile: true
---

This research examines how long-term fire suppression reshapes bistability and resilience in boreal forest–grassland systems. Boreal landscapes often contain both forest and grassland states, and fire is a key disturbance process that influences transitions between them. Changes in fire regimes can therefore alter not only vegetation composition, but also the stability structure of the entire landscape.

The study uses regions with contrasting fire-management histories to ask how weakened fire feedbacks influence forest–grassland alternative states. In systems where fire remains active, recurrent burning can help maintain open grassland states and reinforce the separation between forest and grassland. Under long-term fire suppression, this feedback is weakened, potentially changing the conditions under which the two vegetation states coexist.

A major focus of this work is to understand whether fire suppression weakens hysteresis between forest and grassland states while altering ecosystem resilience. By combining remote sensing, field observations, and dynamical analysis, this research evaluates how vegetation states, recovery dynamics, and transition risks differ between fire-suppressed and fire-active landscapes.

This work highlights disturbance regulation as a key mechanism shaping ecosystem stability. Fire suppression may reduce the strength of historical feedbacks that maintained forest–grassland bistability, while also changing the recovery capacity of vegetation under climate change. More broadly, this research contributes to understanding how human alteration of disturbance regimes can reorganize alternative stable states and resilience in high-latitude ecosystems.
